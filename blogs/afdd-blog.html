AFDD: The Device That Stops Electrical Fires Before They Start

A plain-language guide to Arc Fault Detection Devices — what they are, how they work, and why every responsible electrical installation needs one.

Every year, hundreds of industrial and commercial fires in India are caused by a silent, invisible hazard — arc faults inside electrical wiring. The surprising part? Your existing MCBs and RCCBs cannot detect them. That's exactly what an AFDD (Arc Fault Detection Device) is designed to fix.

🤔 What is an AFDD? Think of it this way...

🏠 A Simple Analogy: Gas Pipeline vs. Gas Detector

Your home has a gas pipeline. You have a pressure relief valve (like an MCB) — it activates only if there is a massive leak. But what about a tiny pinhole leak that is too small to trigger the valve, yet slowly fills a room with gas?

An Arc Fault Detection Device (AFDD) is a smart protective device fitted in your distribution board or panel. It continuously "listens" to the electrical current flowing through your wires and detects the unique pattern created by dangerous arcing. The moment it finds one, it shuts off the circuit — in less than 5 milliseconds.

AFDDs are standardized under IEC 62606 and are available as combined units with an MCB — offering both arc fault protection and conventional overcurrent protection in a single compact DIN-rail device. Siemens (Aventra's principal partner) manufactures the AFDD 5SM6 series for 230V single-phase circuits.

⚡ What exactly is an "arc fault"?

❌ Normal short circuit (MCB handles this)

Example: A live wire directly touches the earth wire behind your switchboard.

⚡ Massive current surge → MCB trips in milliseconds → circuit is safe.

✓ Your existing MCB was designed exactly for this.

⚠️ Arc fault (MCB cannot detect this)

Example: A wire inside your factory wall is partially chewed by a rat — not fully broken, but damaged.

💥 Current "jumps" the gap → 3,000°C arc → smoulders inside the wall for minutes.

✗ MCB sees almost-normal current — it never trips. Fire starts quietly.

🌡️ Just how hot is 3,000°C?

Steel melts at about 1,370°C. An electrical arc burns at 3,000°C — more than twice that. It can ignite PVC wire insulation, wooden wall studs, and cable duct covers in seconds — while your MCB stays happily in the ON position.

Arc faults happen because of ageing wiring, loose terminal connections, insulation cracked by heat or rodents, over-bent cables, or old plug points. India has enormous amounts of wiring that is 15–30 years old — especially in factories, housing societies, and commercial buildings — making this a real and present danger every day.

🧠 How does an AFDD actually work?

📻 Analogy: FM Radio Signal vs. Static Noise

Your electrical current flows at 50Hz — a clean, regular sine wave, like a clear FM signal. An arc fault creates tiny rapid "crackling" vibrations on top of that wave — like static noise breaking into your FM signal. A conventional MCB only checks the volume (current level). An AFDD also checks the quality of the waveform — and the moment it detects that "static," it acts.

1

Arc event occurs

Loose terminal, damaged insulation, or degraded cable creates a small arc. Current level stays almost normal — MCB does not trip.

2

Current sensor reads the waveform

The AFDD's built-in sensor reads the current waveform in real time — thousands of samples per second.

3

Digital processor analyses the pattern

A microprocessor compares the waveform against thousands of known arc fault signatures. Smart filtering ignores normal "noise" from motors, dimmers, and inverters — so it does not trip unnecessarily.

4

Circuit disconnected in <5 milliseconds

Arc fault confirmed → breaker trips instantly → fire is prevented before it can start.

❌ Without AFDD

MCB never tripped. RCCB never tripped. Nobody knew.

✓ With AFDD

The AFDD on that MCC outgoing feeder trips on Day 1 of the arcing. The electrician finds the loose terminal next morning, tightens it in 10 minutes. Panel back online. Total downtime: 45 minutes.

❌ Without AFDD

Building is 18 years old, wiring never replaced. A cracked wire behind a false ceiling starts arcing near an AC unit. AC keeps working normally. Three months later, a slow fire destroys the entire floor — servers, documents, workstations.

💡 Key Insight

In all three scenarios, the MCBs and RCCBs were perfectly functional and doing their jobs correctly. Arc faults are simply outside what those devices were ever designed to detect. AFDD doesn't replace your MCB — it adds the one layer of protection that nothing else provides.

⚠️ Common mistakes — and what to do instead

❌ Mistake #1: "I have MCB and RCCB — that's enough"

Example: A warehouse manager installs the best MCBs and RCCBs money can buy — but his wiring is 18 years old in unknown condition.

⚠️ Problem: Neither device detects arc faults. Both will work perfectly while a fire starts inside the wall.

✓ Fix: Add AFDD to circuits in older buildings or circuits with long cable runs that can't be easily inspected.

❌ Mistake #2: "AFDD will keep tripping because of my motors"

Example: Engineer avoids AFDD on motor circuits, fearing nuisance trips every time a motor starts.

⚠️ Problem: Modern AFDDs (like Siemens 5SM6) have intelligent filtering built in. They distinguish arc fault signatures from motor inrush, VFD harmonics, and dimmer noise.

✓ Fix: Use an AFDD rated for your load type. Aventra's team can help specify the right model.

❌ Mistake #3: "AFDD is only for homes, not factories"

Example: Plant manager dismisses AFDD as a "residential product" and skips it for the factory distribution board.

⚠️ Problem: Industrial environments have MORE arc fault risk — longer cable runs, mechanical vibration loosening terminals, rodents in cable trays, ageing wiring.

✓ Fix: Prioritise AFDD on MCC outgoing feeders, lighting circuits, and office/control room circuits in industrial facilities.

📍 Where should AFDD be installed?

✓ High Priority Circuits

  • • Circuits feeding areas with old or concealed wiring
  • • Office areas, server rooms, control rooms
  • • Sleeping areas (hotels, hospitals, hostels)
  • • Lighting circuits with long cable runs
  • • Circuits where wiring inspection is difficult
  • • Critical process circuits where downtime is expensive

📌 Practical Notes

  • • AFDD occupies 2 DIN rail modules — plan panel space accordingly
  • • Must be wired load-side after the supply terminals
  • • Test button must be accessible for periodic testing
  • • Siemens 5SM6: rated for 230V, 6kA breaking capacity
  • • Combined RCBO+AFDD gives most complete protection

📜 What do the standards say?

  • • IEC 62606 — International standard defining AFDD performance, sensitivity, and testing requirements.
  • • BS 7671 UK 18th Edition — Mandates AFDD in bedrooms and sleeping areas in new and significantly modified installations.
  • • India — IS 732 / National Building Code — Not yet mandated, but alignment with IEC standards is progressing. Leading industrial clients and EPC contractors already specify AFDD for fire risk management and insurance compliance.
